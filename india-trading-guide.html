01
Foundation

NSE vs BSE — India's Two Exchanges

Before placing your first trade, you need to know where stocks are traded in India.

India has two major stock exchanges. Both list the same large-cap stocks, but they differ in history, benchmarks, and what traders typically use.

NSE · Preferred by traders
National Stock Exchange
  • Founded 1992 — newer but bigger volume
  • Benchmark: Nifty 50 (top 50 companies)
  • Better liquidity for most stocks
  • Where intraday traders prefer to operate
  • Suffix to use: RELIANCE.NS
BSE · Oldest in Asia
Bombay Stock Exchange
  • Founded 1875 — oldest in Asia
  • Benchmark: Sensex (top 30 companies)
  • More small/mid-cap listings
  • Good for long-term investors
  • Suffix to use: RELIANCE.BO
Beginner Tip

As a beginner, focus only on Nifty 50 stocks on NSE. These are India's 50 largest, most liquid companies. High liquidity means you can enter and exit trades easily without price slippage. Avoid penny stocks and unknown small-caps until you have experience.

What is Nifty 50?

The Nifty 50 is NSE's flagship index — a basket of India's 50 largest companies by market capitalisation. When people say "the market went up today", they usually mean Nifty 50 moved up. It's the pulse of the Indian economy. If Nifty is falling, most stocks fall with it. If Nifty is rising, most stocks rise.

Key Fact

Nifty 50 is dominated by Banking (30%), IT (15%), and Energy (12%). When FIIs sell banking stocks, Nifty often falls even if other sectors are fine. Always watch banking stocks and RELIANCE — they move the index most.

02
Operations

Market Timings & Sessions

Indian markets run on IST (Indian Standard Time, UTC+5:30). All times below are IST.

8:00
SGX Nifty / Gift Nifty Opens Singapore exchange trades Indian futures. Gives early indication of where Nifty will open. Check this before market opens.
9:00
Pre-Open Session Begins Orders are collected but not executed. Price discovery happens here. The opening price is set based on pre-open orders.
9:15
🔔 Market Opens — Trading Begins This is the most volatile 15 minutes of the day. Intraday traders watch the 9:15–9:30 AM range very closely (Opening Range Breakout strategy).
11:30
Mid-Session Lull Volume and volatility typically reduce. Not ideal for intraday trades. Good time to review your open positions.
2:30
Afternoon Rally / Sell-off Volume picks up again. US pre-market opens, influencing IT and global stocks. Intraday positions should ideally be squared off here.
3:30
🔕 Market Closes All intraday positions must be squared off before this. After 3:30 PM, no new intraday orders. Post-close session for delivery trades until 4:00 PM.
Critical for Intraday Traders

If you have an intraday (MIS) position open at 3:20 PM, your broker will auto-square it off at market price — often unfavourable. Always square off intraday positions before 3:15 PM to stay in control of your exit price.

03
Market Forces

Who Moves Indian Markets?

Understanding who the big players are helps you trade with the trend, not against it.

FII — Foreign Institutional Investors

Foreign funds (American, European, Japanese) that invest in Indian stocks. They manage trillions of dollars and their flows are the single biggest driver of Nifty direction on any given day.

When FIIs Buy
Bullish Signal 📈
  • Nifty typically rises
  • Large-cap stocks benefit most
  • INR strengthens (more $ coming in)
  • Often happens when US dollar weakens
When FIIs Sell
Bearish Signal 📉
  • Nifty often falls even if India is fine
  • INR weakens ($ going out)
  • Happens when US rates rise (better returns abroad)
  • Can cause 2–5% corrections in weeks

DII — Domestic Institutional Investors

Indian mutual funds, LIC, and insurance companies. They act as a cushion — when FIIs sell, DIIs often buy, limiting the downside. India's domestic SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) inflows now exceed ₹20,000 crore per month, making DIIs a permanent stabilising force in the market.

Retail Traders (You)

Individual investors and traders like you. Retail accounts for about 45% of trading volume but typically follows institutional flows rather than leading them. Smart retail trading means identifying what FIIs and DIIs are doing and aligning with their direction.

Where to Check FII/DII Data Daily

Go to nseindia.com → Market Data → FII/DII Activity. This is published every evening after market close. Check it every day — it takes 2 minutes and gives you massive context for the next day's trading.

RBI — Reserve Bank of India

India's central bank. Its Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) meets 6 times a year. The repo rate (what rate RBI lends to banks) is the most watched number. Rate hikes hurt banking and real estate stocks. Rate cuts benefit them. The announcement date is always a high-volatility day.

04
Technical Analysis

The 6 Indicators Every Beginner Needs

Don't use more than 2–3 at a time. More indicators = more confusion, not more accuracy.

RSI
Relative Strength Index
Measures how fast price is moving. Ranges 0–100. Best on 14-period setting.
RSI < 30 = Oversold → Buy opportunity
RSI > 70 = Overbought → Exit signal
EMA
Exponential Moving Average
Tracks average price, giving more weight to recent candles. Faster to react than SMA.
Fast EMA crosses above Slow EMA = Buy
Fast EMA crosses below Slow EMA = Sell
MACD
Moving Avg Convergence Divergence
Shows momentum. Uses 12, 26, 9 EMA combo. Look for MACD line crossing signal line.
MACD crosses above signal line = Bullish
MACD crosses below = Bearish
VWAP
Volume Weighted Avg Price
Average price weighted by volume. Resets daily. Used by institutional traders to evaluate entry quality.
Price above VWAP = Bullish intraday
Price below VWAP = Bearish intraday
ATR
Average True Range
Measures volatility — how much a stock moves on average. Critical for setting stop losses intelligently.
Stop Loss = Entry − (1.5 × ATR)
High ATR = wider SL needed
S/R
Support & Resistance
Price levels where stocks historically reverse. Not a formula — identified by eye on charts.
Buy near support, place SL just below
Sell near resistance or when support breaks
The Indicator Trap

Beginners often add 5–6 indicators hoping for "certainty." This creates analysis paralysis — conflicting signals that paralyse decisions. Start with just EMA (trend) + RSI (momentum). That's the combo used in your backtester and it's more than enough to start.

05
Trading Strategies

Your Three Starter Strategies

One for each style — intraday, swing, and long-term. Master these before adding others.

Intraday
⚡
Opening Range Breakout (ORB)
Note the High and Low of the first 15 minutes (9:15–9:30 AM). Trade the breakout from this range with volume confirmation.
  • Mark the 9:15–9:30 AM high and low on your chart
  • If price breaks above the high with volume surge → BUY
  • If price breaks below the low with volume surge → SHORT
  • Stop loss = other end of the opening range
  • Target = 1.5× to 2× the range width
  • Best on: RELIANCE, HDFCBANK, TCS, Bank Nifty
  • Exit by 3:00 PM regardless
Swing
〰
EMA Crossover (9/21 or 20/50)
Use the daily chart. When the faster EMA crosses above the slower EMA, a new uptrend is beginning. Hold the trade for days to weeks.
  • Plot 9 EMA and 21 EMA on daily chart
  • Wait for 9 EMA to cross above 21 EMA → BUY
  • Confirm with RSI between 45–60 (not overbought)
  • Stop loss = below recent swing low or 21 EMA
  • Target = 1.5× to 2× your risk
  • Hold for 5–15 days typically
  • Exit when 9 EMA crosses back below 21 EMA
Long Term
🌱
Quality Stocks on Dip
Great companies don't stay cheap. When Nifty 50 quality stocks fall 8–15% due to market correction (not company problems), buy in tranches.
  • Only Nifty 50 quality stocks: TCS, HDFCBANK, INFY, HDFC, Titan
  • Wait for a 10%+ pullback from recent highs
  • Verify: is the fall due to market fear or company problem?
  • If market fear only → buy 33% of planned position
  • Buy another 33% if it falls 5% more (average down)
  • Remaining 34% if fundamentals confirmed fine
  • Hold for 12–24 months. Set a reminder.
Paper Trading Rule

For your first 30 paper trades, only use one strategy at a time. If you trade ORB intraday, don't also take a swing trade that day. Mixing strategies confuses your learning. Track which strategy each trade uses in your journal.

06
Capital Preservation

Risk Management — The Only Rule That Matters

Strategies win money. Risk management keeps you alive long enough to let the strategy work.

Most beginners blow their accounts not because of bad strategies, but because they took one large position and lost everything on it. The 1% rule prevents this.

Live Example — The 1% Rule in Action
Paper Capital₹1,00,000
Max Risk Per Trade (1%)₹1,000
Trade: Buy HDFCBANK at ₹1,650—
Stop Loss at ₹1,635 (₹15 below entry)SL = ₹1,635
Risk per share₹15
Position Size = ₹1,000 ÷ ₹1566 shares
Total Trade Value = 66 × ₹1,650₹1,08,900
If SL hit → Loss = 66 × ₹15−₹990
Capital remaining after SL hit₹99,010 (99%)

You can lose 10 trades in a row and still have 90% of your capital. That's survivability. Compare this to risking 10% per trade — after just 3 losses in a row, you'd be down 27%.

Risk:Reward Ratio

Never take a trade where you risk more than you expect to gain. The minimum acceptable R:R is 1:1.5. Aim for 1:2 or better.

Good Trade Setup
1 : 2 Risk Reward
  • Entry: ₹500. Risk: ₹10 (SL at ₹490)
  • Target: ₹520 (₹20 gain)
  • Even with 40% win rate → profitable
  • 10 trades: 4 wins (₹800) 6 losses (−₹600)
  • Net: +₹200 profit with 40% accuracy
Bad Trade Setup
1 : 0.5 Risk Reward
  • Entry: ₹500. Risk: ₹20 (SL at ₹480)
  • Target: ₹510 (₹10 gain only)
  • Need 70%+ win rate just to break even
  • 10 trades: 6 wins (₹600) 4 losses (−₹800)
  • Net: −₹200 loss with 60% accuracy

Before Every Trade — Checklist

  • Do I know exactly where my stop loss is?
  • Have I calculated my position size using the 1% rule?
  • Is my R:R ratio at least 1:1.5?
  • Is there a major news event today that could spike volatility?
  • Is the overall market trend aligned with my trade direction?
  • Am I trading because of a clear signal, not because I'm bored?

What Triggers Each Sector

Banking: RBI repo rate decisions. Rising rates = margin pressure. Falling rates = sector rallies. Also watch credit growth data and NPA (bad loan) numbers.

IT: US economy and USD/INR rate. When dollar strengthens, Indian IT companies earn more in rupee terms. US recession fears = IT sector selloff in India.

Pharma: US FDA approvals/rejections. A single FDA warning letter can drop a pharma stock 15% in one day. Also watch generic drug price pressure in the US market.

Auto: RBI interest rates (EMI affordability), fuel prices, monsoon season (rural demand), and EV transition news.

FMCG: Monsoon performance (rural consumption), input cost inflation (palm oil, wheat prices), and urban consumer sentiment.

Glossary of Indian Market Terms

Terms you'll encounter in news, on trading platforms, and in this system.

Intraday / MIS
Buy and sell the same stock within the same trading day. Must be squared off before 3:30 PM. "MIS" (Margin Intraday Square-off) is how brokers call it.
Delivery / CNC
Buy and hold overnight or longer. "CNC" (Cash and Carry) on Zerodha/Groww. Used for swing and long-term trades.
F&O
Futures and Options. Derivatives that derive value from an underlying stock or index. Higher risk, requires more capital. Not recommended for beginners.
Lot Size
In F&O, stocks trade in fixed lot sizes. Nifty lot = 50 units. Bank Nifty lot = 15 units. You can't buy "1 Nifty future" — minimum is 1 lot.
Circuit Breaker
If a stock moves more than 5%, 10%, or 20% in a day, trading is halted temporarily. Index circuit breakers halt entire market at 10%, 15%, 20% moves.
Repo Rate
The rate at which RBI lends to commercial banks. This flows to consumer loan rates (home loans, car loans). Markets watch this obsessively.
Market Cap
Share price × total shares outstanding. Large-cap: >₹20,000 crore. Mid-cap: ₹5,000–20,000 crore. Small-cap: <₹5,000 crore.
SEBI
Securities and Exchange Board of India. The regulator. All brokers, exchanges, and mutual funds must follow SEBI rules. Think of it as India's SEC.
Nifty / Sensex
Nifty 50 = NSE's top 50 stocks index. Sensex = BSE's top 30 stocks index. Both represent overall market health.
Bull / Bear Market
Bull = rising market (20%+ up from lows). Bear = falling market (20%+ down from highs). India has been in a secular bull market since 2003, with bear phases in 2008, 2020.
OHLCV
Open, High, Low, Close, Volume. The 5 data points every candle on a chart shows. The basis of all technical analysis.
Candlestick
A visual representation of OHLCV for a time period. Green candle = price went up. Red candle = price went down. The body shows Open-Close range.
SIP
Systematic Investment Plan. Investing a fixed amount every month regardless of market level. Best long-term strategy for most people. Not directly applicable to stock trading but used in mutual funds.
T+1 Settlement
When you sell a stock, the money hits your account the next trading day (T+1). India moved from T+2 to T+1 in 2023, making it one of the fastest settlement systems globally.
